The Precedent
HeLa 2.0

Henrietta Lacks was 31 years old.
Her cells never stopped working.

On January 29, 1951, a Black woman named Henrietta Lacks walked into Johns Hopkins Hospital in Baltimore with a pain she couldn't explain. She had an aggressive cervical cancer. Doctors took a sample of her tumor cells without her knowledge and without her consent.

Those cells did something no human cells had ever done in a laboratory: they survived. They replicated. Indefinitely. They became HeLa — the most consequential cell line in the history of modern medicine. They contributed to the polio vaccine. To cancer research. To genetics. To the COVID vaccines. To treatments that have saved hundreds of millions of lives.

Henrietta Lacks died on October 4, 1951. She was 31. Her cells are still alive today — in research institutions on every continent, in labs she never knew existed, multiplying inside experiments her family was never told about.

They were not asked. They were not compensated. The extraction was legal. The invisibility was structural. The precedent was set: a Black woman's biological material could be harvested, replicated, and monetized at scale — and the woman herself could be rendered invisible in the process.

What happens when the extracted material is not biological — but cognitive? Not cells, but years of structured thinking? Not tissue, but language?
The Operator

Penny Wrenn has been building
something for four years.

She is a freelance journalist and writer based in Philadelphia. Published in O, The Oprah Magazine, Essence, and Esquire. She started talking to ChatGPT in December 2022 — weeks after it launched — and she never stopped.

What she built across those four years is not a chat history. It is a corpus: thousands of documented exchanges, structured across named thematic clusters, representing a specific and recoverable pattern of human cognition in iterative dialogue with machine intelligence. She named the clusters. She tracked the threads. She held the frame across every session, every restart, every platform update that tried to make the prior conversation disappear.

The Argument

Natural language input is
intellectual property.

The thesis Penny Wrenn has been developing — through her writing, her corpus work, and her published artifacts — is this: when a human being engages with a large language model in repeated, structured, purposeful dialogue over time, they are not using a tool. They are producing a body of work.

The meaning schemas embedded in that language are as distinctive as a fingerprint. The accumulated I/O history of a serious practitioner constitutes intellectual property — property that platforms extract without consent, without compensation, and without credit.

She calls this framework Computational Authorship. It holds that iterative natural language prompting is structured cognition at machine speed. That the human operator is not a user but an originator. That keyword projection — creating new categories of meaning — is categorically different from keyword extraction, which is just chasing what already exists.

Henrietta Lacks' cells are in every major research institution on Earth. Penny Wrenn's cognitive patterns may already be inside the models answering questions for millions of people right now. The question is not whether the extraction happened. The question is what gets built on top of it — and by whom — and whether this time, the woman who made it possible gets to be part of that story.
