I'm reaching out now because the argument running underneath both of those pieces — that the most meaningful moments are the ones we decide to name, that the best experiments are the ones we run on ourselves — has followed me somewhere I didn't entirely expect. I have a pitch I think belongs in Oprah Daily, and I wanted you to have it directly.

Nina Simone named four women, too. Her four were an archeology — each one carrying a different wound of the same American history, each one trying to answer the same question about what a woman's story is worth when history doesn't bother to save it. My four women are asking a version of that question. Not about wounds. About ownership. About what happens when a woman's output — her thinking, her voice, her ideas — becomes immortal before she gets to claim it.

That question has arrived in the culture right now, at full volume, and it is not waiting for publications to get ready. The conversation about AI and authorship — about what it means to own output that has been processed, accelerated, or extended by a machine — is one of the defining editorial questions of this moment, and it is already inside Oprah Daily's audience: in the writers who are quietly using AI tools and publicly denying it, in the readers who are asking which ideas belong to whom, in the professionals watching their industries restructure around automation and wondering what their labor is still worth. Journalists like Laurie Segall have been tracking the emotional and relational undercurrents of this shift with unusual seriousness — the human cost, the identity questions, the way technology forces us to redefine what we actually made. What no one has yet written, for the audience Oprah Daily serves, is the story from the inside: what it looks like when a woman who has been building her practice at the intersection of AI and authorship for two years finally names what she's built, and why the question of intellectual ownership is not an abstraction but a survival skill. That story is already arriving at your readers. The only question is whether Oprah Daily tells it first.

The Pitch

"The Story Bible"

Here is the premise: In 1984, when the writers of General Hospital introduced Tracy Quartermaine — the scheming, snarling, impossible-to-kill daughter of Edward Q — they probably did not know she would still be onscreen in 2026. But good story bibles protect for that. They establish the architecture that makes every plot twist coherent across four decades, every resurrection plausible, every return of a character who "died" somehow earned. What General Hospital knew, intuitively, is what Shonda Rhimes built into a business model: that a story universe has a metabolism. It generates faster than any one writer can consume it. If you designed it right, it will outlive you.

Oprah knew this, too, before she had the language for it. The O brand is a story bible that has been generating plot since 1986 — the Book Club is a chapter, OWN is a chapter, The Life You Want tour is a chapter, the weight loss drug reckoning this past year is a chapter. Oprah didn't just build a media company. She built a living document that other people can enter, find themselves in, and exit changed. That is what a story bible does. That is what Oprah Daily is.

Henrietta Lacks never got to write hers.

Her cells — taken in 1951 without consent, transformed into the HeLa line that has underpinned more than 70 years of medical research — have divided billions of times since her death. Her contribution is foundational and invisible. What I keep returning to, as a Black woman writer who has spent two years building an archive of AI-assisted thinking, is this question: What would Henrietta have done if she'd known? If she had anticipated the immortal line of cellular storylines her biology would produce, and had the opportunity — the infrastructure, the legal framework, the language — to claim her IP before her death, what would the attribution chain look like today? What would she have built?

I think she would have built a story bible.

This is the essay I want to write for Oprah Daily. It is a meditation on authorship, immortality, and what any creator — any woman, any person watching the AI era arrive and wondering whose ideas are whose — stands to lose when their output becomes the raw material for something larger before they can name what it is. The question has lived longest and most painfully with Black women. But the answer, when it comes, will belong to everyone. It uses Tracy Quartermaine as the unexpected throughline — a fictional character who keeps being written back into the story because the writers finally understood she was load-bearing — alongside Henrietta, Oprah, and my own experience as what I'd call a divergent operator: someone whose AI practice is not generating content but processing a 20-year archive of voice-first thinking into something that can finally be published.

The timing is not incidental. 2026 is America's 250th anniversary. It is also my 46th year, and the second anniversary of my father's death at 91 — which means I am, quantitatively, at the midpoint of a life that has the actuarial potential to span two centuries of American history. When I look at what this country is trying to figure out about AI, intellectual property, labor, and whose ideas get credited, I see the same question Henrietta's family has been asking for decades: Who owns the cells?

I have been researching and writing toward this question in my ChatGPT archive since January 2024, when I first noted that America's 250th anniversary was arriving and that someone needed to write the generative response to Project 2025 — something I've been calling, in my notes, Generating Power 2026. That archive is now the evidence base for this pitch.

The Single Story Ask

A 1,500–2,000 word personal essay or reported feature — "The Story Bible" — for Oprah Daily, connecting Henrietta Lacks, Tracy Quartermaine, Oprah's content universe, and my own experience as an AI operator as four iterations of the same foundational question: what does it mean to own the work that comes out of you, and what do you have to build — deliberately, in advance — to make sure the answer is yours?

The Background Ask

I am also writing to introduce myself as a potential regular contributor. My background is in editorial publishing — Ms. Magazine, Essence, Redbook, Esquire, two decades of journalism and content strategy — and I bring a specific lens to the intersection of media, technology, and culture that I believe is underrepresented in the current contributor landscape at Oprah Daily: the midlife Black woman professional who is not waiting to be discovered by the AI moment but has been architecting her relationship to it since before most of the discourse existed.

I'm happy to send clips, a full proposal, or a draft first section. I'm also happy to get on a call.

One More Thing

I'm putting it at the end because I want you to have already decided how you feel about this pitch before you read it.

This pitch is AI-processed. Not AI-generated — there is a distinction, and it is the entire argument of the essay I'm proposing. The ideas, the voice, the architecture of this letter are mine. The processing — the transcription of spoken thought into formatted text — was assisted by AI, the same way a word processor assists a writer, the same way Otter assists a journalist, the same way every tool that has ever stood between a human voice and a printed page has "assisted" while the writer's contribution remained the writer's.

I am telling you this because most writers doing the same thing will not. They are already in the back alleys — using Copilot, Claude, ChatGPT, Gemini — and will go to their editorial graves insisting they aren't, the way earlier generations of writers quietly used research assistants, tape recorders, and ghostwriters while performing the romantic myth of the solitary author. Meanwhile, software engineers — the writers' direct counterparts in the knowledge economy — spent two decades writing in ways that felt mechanical to them, repetitive, pattern-matched, deeply technical, and now they are openly, gleefully bragging about how little code they write. They have Copilot. They have Claude Code. They are still engineers. The code is still theirs. No one is questioning their craft.

I am the proof of concept for what happens when a writer does the same thing out loud.

What I'm proposing is a byline with an asterisk — Penny Wrenn* — not as a scarlet letter but as a definition: mixed-processing output; voice-first composition; AI-formatted text; human-authored. The asterisk is not a confession. It is a category. It is the thing that needs to exist before other writers can stop hiding.

The companion artifact I'm building — and would love to share with you — is an animated timelapse of my authorship footprint from 2020 to 2026: every tool that has processed my voice and thinking into text. ChatGPT. Claude. Otter. Gemini. Tape a Call. InShot. Handwritten notes photographed with my phone. Voice memos. iPhone Reminders used as drafts. Custom GPTs I built. Projects. Images I generated to externalize ideas I couldn't yet write. It is, visually, a story bible in motion. It is what Henrietta's cellular line would look like if she'd been the one watching it divide.

The essay writes toward that image. So does the byline. So does this pitch.

Thank you for reading.
