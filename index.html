That instinct — the inventory instinct — is the origin of everything that follows.

May 20, 2024 — The Proof

The moment the argument
made itself.

A job interview. Director of Content and News Media. The interviewer said the role required "writing horsepower." He was not convinced that AI-assisted writing qualified.

The Rejection

"Writing horsepower. The output required of an engine or motor, not of a person."

What she wrote back — via voice-to-text — as proof

"I don't believe that writing only exists because it is typed. I am writing with words when I speak them all the time. And that is how I write with AI. As evidenced even in this input-output exchange right now.

If the written word is so precious — then we're saying people like Stephen Hawking didn't write. We are saying that people who are blind cannot write. We are saying that someone who has lost the function of their hands can't write.

What if we confined the written word to handwriting? Then to typed text? Then what if autogenerated captions — which are indelible to modern media literacy — or autocorrect, or Grammarly? Each new tool met with the same anxiety. Each time, the writing survived."

This message was composed via voice-to-text. The proof was in the prompt. The corpus is the argument. The AI was the word processor. Penny Wrenn was the writer.

She coined the distinction that day: AI-processed, not AI-generated. Not as defensive positioning. As a technical description of what was actually happening.

The industry spent the following year debating a distinction she had already made.

The Argument

Henrietta Lacks was 31 years old.
Her cells never stopped working.

On January 29, 1951, a Black woman named Henrietta Lacks walked into Johns Hopkins Hospital in Baltimore. She had an aggressive cervical cancer. Doctors took a sample of her tumor cells without her knowledge and without her consent.

Those cells became HeLa — the most consequential cell line in the history of modern medicine. They contributed to the polio vaccine. To cancer research. To the COVID vaccines. To treatments that have saved hundreds of millions of lives. Henrietta Lacks died on October 4, 1951. She was 31. Her cells are still alive today.

They were not asked. They were not compensated. The extraction was legal. The invisibility was structural.

Now consider what you just read on this page.
Ask yourself: whose material was it?
Who was the origin?

Penny Wrenn's prompts, her voice, her ideas are the source material that produces the AI output. Every exchange in the corpus is timestamped evidence of that origin. The platforms that received it have used it — openly, by design, as a matter of stated policy — to train systems that now generate language for everyone else.

The corpus doesn't prove she worked hard. It proves she was there first — and that she knew it mattered before anyone told her it did.

That is what the 4,263 conversations are. Not a content library. Not a productivity log. A receipt.

This is HeLa 2.0. Different substrate. Same structure. Same silence — unless the record exists to break it.
